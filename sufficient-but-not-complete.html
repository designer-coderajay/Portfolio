100% sufficient, 22% comprehensive: what ablating a circuit actually tells you

Ajay Pravin Mahale

There is a result in my thesis that looks, at first glance, like a bug in the arithmetic.

I take the six attention heads that the literature identifies as the indirect-object-identification (IOI) circuit in GPT-2 Small. Run the model using only those six heads, and it still produces the correct answer every time. By the standard metric, the circuit is 100% sufficient. Then I ablate those same six heads and measure how far the prediction falls. It should collapse. It doesn't. On average it drops by 22%.

Sufficiency says these heads are enough to do the whole task. The ablation says the model barely notices when they're gone. Both numbers describe the same six heads. If you read them as measuring the same thing, one of them has to be wrong.

Neither is wrong. The gap between them is the finding.

Two questions that sound identical

Sufficiency and comprehensiveness come from ERASER (DeYoung et al., 2020), a benchmark for rationale faithfulness. Applied to a circuit, they ask:

  • Sufficiency. Keep only this circuit, throw everything else away. Does the behavior survive?
  • Comprehensiveness. Remove this circuit, keep everything else. Does the behavior break?

In plain English these feel like the same question asked twice. Mathematically they are two different interventions on two different models. Sufficiency isolates the circuit and asks whether it can carry the task alone. Comprehensiveness deletes the circuit and asks whether the rest of the network can carry the task without it. A component can pass both the "works alone" test and the "not missed when gone" test at once. When it does, that tells you something specific about how the model is built.

It means the model has a backup.

The backup heads

When you ablate the primary name-mover heads in GPT-2 Small, other heads that were sitting quietly in the background wake up and do the job instead. This isn't my observation. It's the backup name-mover behavior documented in the original IOI work (Wang et al., 2022). My contribution is measuring what that redundancy does to a faithfulness score, and the measurement is blunt: it caps comprehensiveness at 22% no matter how clean the circuit looks.

I have a sharper piece of evidence that these backup heads are genuinely redundant rather than secretly part of the core. One candidate head, L10H10, fires on 82% of prompts, often enough that you'd be tempted to fold it into the circuit. I tried. Adding it moved the circuit's faithfulness F1 from 36.0% to 34.4%. Including the backup head made the explanation worse.

That number is the whole argument in miniature. A redundant mechanism doesn't add explanatory power when you include it, because it wasn't doing the work in the first place. It was standing by in case the work stopped getting done. The 6-head circuit was already the right cut. The 22% ceiling isn't noise to be engineered away. It's the model telling you the computation is spread out and fault-tolerant, and no single clean circuit will ever be comprehensive on its own.

Why this matters past GPT-2

The specific numbers here are narrow on purpose: one task (IOI), one model (GPT-2 Small), fifty prompts. I don't claim they generalize, and anyone who tells you a toy-model circuit result transfers cleanly to a frontier model is guessing. What I think does travel is the methodological point.

If you evaluate an explanation with a single faithfulness metric, you will overstate it. Sufficiency alone would let me publish "the IOI circuit fully explains this behavior" with a straight face and a 100% number behind it. The comprehensiveness measurement is what stops me. It exposes the redundancy that sufficiency structurally cannot see. The two metrics disagree exactly where the interesting structure lives, and the disagreement is the thing worth reporting.

This connects to the other half of my thesis, which found near-zero correlation (r = 0.009) between how confident GPT-2 is in an answer and how faithful its explanation of that answer turns out to be. Same lesson from a different angle. The properties we reach for as proxies for trust, a confident logit or a sufficient circuit, are not measuring what we hope they measure. You have to intervene on the model to find out, and you have to intervene in more than one way.

The honest caveat

The framing I keep using, "100% sufficient yet only 22% comprehensive," is a good headline and a slightly dangerous one. It invites exactly the misreading I opened with, that the two numbers contradict each other. They don't. They are answers to two different counterfactuals, and a reader who knows ERASER will check that I know that. I do, and the distinction is the reason the result is worth writing down. A circuit that is sufficient but not comprehensive isn't a failed explanation. It's an accurate map of a model that kept a spare.

Method notes: GPT-2 Small, IOI task, n = 50 prompts. Circuit heads L9H6, L9H9, L10H0 and the S-inhibition and duplicate-token heads upstream, following Wang et al. (2022). Faithfulness scored with ERASER sufficiency/comprehensiveness (DeYoung et al., 2020); F1 is their harmonic mean. Comprehensiveness mean 22.0% (std 17.3%). Confidence and comprehensiveness correlation r = 0.009. Code and the interactive circuit diagram are on the site and in the thesis repo.
